Understanding ADHD: The Basics

ADHD, or Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder, is a neurodevelopmental condition that affects focus, self-control, and energy regulation. Though the name is fairly recent, similar behaviors were described over a century ago, and today ADHD is recognized as a common condition that can begin in childhood and continue into adulthood, influencing school, work, and daily life.

The 3 Types of ADHD Explained

ADHD isn’t the same for everyone — in fact, it’s grouped into three main types. The Inattentive Type is marked by challenges with focus and organization, the Hyperactive-Impulsive Type involves high energy and impulsive behavior, and the Combined Type includes features of both. Knowing the differences helps people understand how ADHD can show up in unique ways.

Predominately Hyperactive-Implusive Presentation

The Hyperactive-Impulsive Type of ADHD is defined by high energy, restlessness, and acting quickly without thinking things through. People with this type may fidget, talk a lot, interrupt others, or have trouble sitting still, especially in situations that require patience. Because the behaviors are more visible and disruptive, this type is often noticed earlier in children, but it can affect people of all ages.

Predominately Inattentive Presentation

The Inattentive Type of ADHD is often harder to spot because it doesn’t usually involve hyperactive behavior. Instead, it’s marked by challenges like losing focus, being easily distracted, forgetting details, and struggling with organization. People with this type may seem quiet or daydreamy, which can sometimes cause their difficulties to be overlooked, especially in school or work settings.Because they’re usually not disruptive, this type can sometimes go unnoticed, especially in girls or adults.

Combined Presentation

The Combined Type of ADHD is the most common presentation and includes symptoms of both inattention and hyperactivity-impulsivity. People with this type may find it hard to stay organized, remember details, or focus on tasks, while also feeling restless, energetic, or prone to acting without thinking. Because it involves challenges in both areas, the combined type can have a big impact on daily life, but understanding it makes it easier to find the right strategies and support.

Diagnostic Requirements

  1. Number of symptoms:
    • Children < 17 years: at least 6 symptoms of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity.
    • Adolescents & adults (17+): at least 5 symptoms in either category.
  2. Duration: Symptoms present for at least 6 months.
  3. Onset: Several symptoms were present before age 12.
  4. Settings: Symptoms occur in two or more settings (e.g., home, school, work, social situations).
  5. Impact: Symptoms clearly interfere with or reduce quality of social, academic, or occupational functioning.
  6. Exclusion: Symptoms are not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., anxiety, depression, a learning disorder, or a medical condition).

Note: This is a summary for informational purposes. Formal diagnosis should be made by a qualified clinician.
